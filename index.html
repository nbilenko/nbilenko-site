Dr. Natalia Bilenko

I am a freelance machine learning engineer, experienced with image and video data (scientific and medical), predictive modeling, and data visualization. I am particularly interested in algorithm interpretability, opacity, and fairness. My goal is to create more equitable algorithms and algorithmic applications.

From 2016 to 2019, I worked as a machine learning engineer at Caption Health (formerly Bay Labs), designing algorithms and software for detection and diagnosis of heart disease based on ultrasound imaging. I co-created the company diversity and inclusion working group and helped start the internship program. I also co-managed the global outreach program, through which we collaborated with the nonprofit organization Team Heart.

Before that, I received a PhD from the Helen Wills Neuroscience Institute at UC Berkeley, where I was supervised by Jack Gallant. For my disseration, I modeled similarities across people's brain responses to movies and speech to study how different kinds of information are represented in the brain. While at Berkeley, I received a Masters in computer science, advised by Maneesh Agrawala. My thesis project was a web app for creating custom visualizations of texts that reflected character relationships and emotional trajectories.

Advocacy and volunteering

I am an organizer for Queer in AI, a group working to raise awareness of queer issues in machine learning and to forge a community of queer researchers. Our next event is a workshop at NeurIPS 2019 in Vancouver.

Art and design

I love scientific art and visualization, and I'm hoping to find time for a new collaborative art project soon.

I was part of the team that created Dr. Brainlove, an interactive climbable brain sculpture with EEG-based programmable lights. Dr. Brainlove went to Burning Man twice and participated in the Bay Area Science Festival, and I moderated a panel on Dr. Brainlove at South by Southwest Interactive.

For an exhibition at the Exploratorium on cognitive technologies, I co-designed an experience called the Mind's Eye that allowed visitors to interactively explore predictive modeling and decoding of brain activity via motion sensing.

As a graduate student at Berkeley, I was a designer for the popular science magazine Berkeley Science Review and led development for our first mobile app.

I'm learning how to draw! Advice welcome.
